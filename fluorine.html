Fluorine


History, Common Uses, and Fun Facts

Fluorine is most commonly associated with dental hygiene, but has uses and applications that extend far beyond this. HF, or hydrofluoric acid, is an extremely dangerous and corrosive chemical, and is commonly used to etch glass. Similarly, fluorohydrocarbons are frequently found in air conditioning and refrigeration units; however, these molecules are notorious greenhouse gases, and have been replaced with more environmentaly-friendly species. For dental applications, fluorine is dissolved in water - this is fluoride - and used to prevent cavities. In fact, many cities added trace levels of fluoride to the water supply, to support city-wide dental health. Fluorine was discovered in 1529 by Georigius Agricola, but was not isolated until 1866 by Ferinand Frederick Henri Moissan, a French chemist. For this, Moissan recieved the Nobel Prize in 1906. It's name is derived from the Latin and French word fluere, which translates to flow or flux. It is extremely reactive; in fact, it is the most electronegative (e.g. reactive) element. When isolated, it is pale yellow, diatomic, highly corrosive, and flammable. In the presence of water, it reacts violently to produce oxygen and hydrofluoric acid, and extremely dangerous and potentially fatal chemical.
