Argon


History, Common Uses, and Fun Facts

Often mistaken for Neon, Argon is the third most abundant gas in Earth's atmosphere; at 0.934%, it is twice as abundant as water vapor, 23 times more abundant than carbon dioxide, and 500 times more abundant than neon. The name argon comes from the Greek word for lazy and inactive, given that it is extremely unreactive. This is understandable, as it belongs to the family of Noble Gases, which characteristically exhibit a complete octet, or eight electrons, in it's outer atomic shell. In other words, Argon is resistant to reacting and bonding with other elements, as it already contains a stable number of bonding electrons. In industry, Argon is most commonly used as an inert shielding gas in welding, and other high-temperature industrial processes. In these processes, typically unreactive elements may become reactive. It is also used in incandescent and fluorescent lighting and blue-green gas laser. In 1785, it was first suspected to be an atmospheric component by Henry Cavendish, but was not isolated un 1894 by Lord Rayleigh and Sir William Ramsay at University College London. Although Argon is non-toxic and unreactive, it is 38% denser than air. Thus, inhalation of the gas should be avoided, as it is a rapid asphyxiant. In closed areas, this could be problematic, as the gas is colorless, odorless, and tasteless, and as a result, it very difficult to detect.


Safety Concerns

Chemical Contact Acute Health Effect First Aid Measures
Eye Contact Burns or Frostbite Flush eyes with water for 10 minutes.
Inhalation Simple Asphyxiant Relocate to fresh air. Provide mouth-to-mouth, if necessary.
Skin Contact Burns or Frostbite Flush skin with water, and remove contaminated clothing and shoes.
Frostbite Frozen Tissue Warm the tissue and seek medical attention.
