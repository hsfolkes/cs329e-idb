Helium


History, Common Uses, and Fun Facts

Helium is one of the six naturally occuring noble gasses, along with neon, argon, krypton, xnenon and radon. As a noble gas, helium is unlikley to undergo chemical reactions with other elements. This is due to the stability of the helium element, whose outter electron shell is completely full. In fact, Helium is the second least reactive element behind neon. The element was discovered in 1868 by Pierre Janssen and Norman Lockyer. The word helium comes from the Greek word helios meaning sun. Janssen and Norman chose this name because they discovered the element while analyzing sunlight after a solar eclipse. Helium liquifies at a very low temperatrue, -458°F. Therefore, it is commonly used to keep superconductors cool when in operation. In fact, the Large Hadron Collider would not have been able to operate without the use of helium for cooling. Two other key uses of helium are in MRI machines and as a bouyant force. For example, helium is used in blimps and ballons to achieve the desired lift.
