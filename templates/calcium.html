Calcium


History, Common Uses, and Fun Facts

Calcium was discovered in 1808 by Sir Humphry Davy, a Cornish chemist and inventor. Calcium always comes in 5th place. It is the fifth most abudant element after oxygen, silicon, aluminum, and iron (based on mass in the Earth's crust), it is the fifth most abundant ion in seawater, and it is the fifth most abundant element in the human body after oxygen carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen. In our bodies, calcium is mainly found in the bones and teeth. This is why you have most likely been told, and are tired of hearing, that calcium is essential for making bones and teeth strong. In nature, calcium is never found in its isolated form, but rather in compounds. Commonly, it is found in minerals, including limestone (calcium carbonate), gypsum (calcium sulfate), and fluorite (calcium fluoride). To isolate calcium, it must be extracted through electrolysis, such that a direct electrical current is used to separate the element from the compound. Once extracted, calcium is extremely reactive, and will almost immediately form a grayish-white oxide and nitride layer when exposed to air. Calcium comes from the Latin word "calx", which means lime; it was given this name not because of any resemblance to the popular fruit, but rather due to its presence in the previously mentioned mineral -- limestone.
